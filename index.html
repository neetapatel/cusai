More...

This course introduces students to graphic design as both an art medium and a form of communication. Students will produce communicative work in multiple formats (posters! zines! animations!) and explore visual problem-solving methods through a variety of in-class exercises. Participation in workshops, group discussions, guest lectures, and critiques will provide students with opportunities to develop their work with each other.



Monday, July 10

Introduction

Exercise: Dots & Lines (starts)

DOTS: Within an 8 x 8-inch square area and using a fine-tipped pencil or pen, fill the entire surface of the square with as many evenly spaced points as possible freehand. The goal is to populate the given area, using hand/eye coordination only, to create distinct and evenly distributed spacing around each point. It may take several attempts to get it done to your satisfaction.

LINES 1: Using the same set-up as above (8 x 8-inch square), draw as many evenly spaced straight lines as possible across the square freehand; lines should be parallel to one edge of your square.

LINES 2: Repeat, but draw lines on the diagonal across the square freehand.

FORMAT: You may want to experiment with different densities (more or less white space). Please organize and save all sketches. The final pieces should be trimmed to size.

Exercise: Type Journal (starts)

As you’re out and about during the program, take note of interesting, attractive, ugly, or otherwise notable typography in your local environments. Take pictures and collect them in our class Are.na channel. We’ll come back to the channel as a group now and then to talk about what you’re seeing.

Project: Poster Series (starts)

Create a series of three 24” x 36” visually-related posters that engage and respond to a subject you’re interested in. The goal is to conceptually and visually interpret, make yours, and communicate the main ideas, questions, and positions you are curious about in your subject. What do you want to say?

Consider both analog and digital ways of making as you begin to brainstorm a list of potential topics. Maybe you will research images online, and collect typography to collage in from magazines and other ephemera. Maybe you will use the scanner to manipulate your analog work, digitize it, and/or reproduce it.

Wednesday, July 12

Image-making

Critique: Dots & Lines (finishes)

We will conduct an brief critique, really more of a share, of this week's in-class exercises. Be prepared at the start of class with your final pieces, please!

Workshop: Photoshop

Neeta will conduct a general workshop on image manipulation in Photoshop. We will cover: masking, collaging, image editing, and the basic functionality of the software. By this point, you should have begun your posters, and I hope you will have questions queued up!

Project: Poster Series (continues)

We will spend most of class working on the poster series. If time allows, we’ll end class with a short pin-up to discuss your work.

Project: Zine (starts)

Choose a Wikipedia page on something you’re fascinated by. Your topic should be related, however directly or loosely, to the subject matter of your posters. Design a pamphlet using all the text and images. Consider other forms of text as well, such as footnotes, hyperlinks, references, external links, etc. What opportunities do they present? How can they be integrated, emphasized, highlighted, or made visible or invisible? Begin topic selection and research collection for next week!

The Wikipedia page should be your foundation, but feel free to incorporate other forms of media and imagery to support your publication. The recommended page size for your zine is 5x8 inches, but you can also pick your own. You are required to make two copies.
